Structural macroeconomic forecasting using a text-based measure of policy uncertainty

By Renato Vassallo

April 12, 2023

This study employs a text-based uncertainty indicator within a Bayesian VAR framework to conduct robust structural macroeconomic forecasting. Our analysis utilizes monthly US data on Economic Policy Uncertainty (EPU), Industrial Production, Consumption, and Employment spanning the period from 1985 to 2019. Subsequently, we construct structural forecasts by assuming a predetermined trajectory for EPU, starting from March 2023. To account for the inherent uncertainty surrounding the model parameters, we propose a Bayesian estimation method employing independent Normal-Wishart priors. By incorporating these methodological advancements, we enhance the robustness and credibility of our macroeconomic forecasting framework.

Main findings

  • Bayesian VAR framework: Our approach is capable of updating and modifying the prior beliefs, finding distributions strongly centered on negative values for coefficients associated with the impact of EPU first lag on the endogenous variables.
  • Impulse response analysis: the Cholesky decomposition is performed assuming a recursive ordering where the first variable is the EPU. Unexpected shocks of policy uncertainty are found to affect the real aggregates of the US economy significantly and persistently.
  • Forecasting: an unconditional and structural forecast exercise is carried out assuming that the EPU follows a fixed future path. While the unconditional forecast presents a mean-reverting behaviour typical of VARs, our conditional forecast allows us to assess the dynamics of real variables driven by pure uncertainty shocks (Rain dance-like).

Impulse Response to an EPU Shock

Figure 1 depicts the model-implied responses of our variables to a 90-point upward EPU innovation, equal in size to the EPU change from its average value in 2005-06 (before the financial crisis) to its average value in 2011-12 (a period with major fiscal policy battles and high EPU levels). We also show the associated 68% credibility region.

The results would indicate that in the face of this unexpected increase in EPU, the three real variables in our model would show not only a significant decline (16th and 84th percentiles are below zero), but also persistent (the shock would dissipate by month 20).

Unconditional Forecast

Using the estimated BVAR, Figure 2 shows the unconditional forecast twelve periods in the future (starting in March 2023). Since we have the complete probability distribution of the forecasts, we present the results as a Fan Chart, with density intervals at 80, 60, 40 and 20 percent, as well as the median value.

When generating an unconditional forecast, it means that we are projecting the variables into the future without considering any specific shocks or external factors. Since the model assumes that the variables revert to their mean over time, the forecast will naturally converge towards the mean values of the variables.

Structural Forecast

We impose that the only shocks that are in the system are uncertainty shocks (EPU ordered first in the structural shock). This will allow us to interpret the forecasted variables in our model as driven by shocks to uncertainty.

In particular, we consider forecasting Y five periods in the future (starting in March 2023). However we impose the condition that the paths from April to August 2023 of the US EPU is the same as during October 2020 to February 2021, i.e., during the last months before and the months during and after the last US national elections.

The presented Fan Charts in Figure 3 give evidence in favour of a probability of around 50% that industrial production would contract for all periods over the projection horizon. Similarly, although with a lower probability (20%), employment would contract from May 2023 onwards. In the case of consumption, its dynamics would be essentially zero for this period.
